FluidOracle generates real-world component performance data as a byproduct of helping engineers make better selections. Here's what that data is, why it matters, and how you can access it.

What FluidOracle does

FluidOracle is an AI-powered, vendor-neutral consulting platform for industrial fluid-path component selection. Engineers describe their operating conditions and problems. The platform asks diagnostic questions, searches curated manufacturer literature and engineering standards, and returns grounded recommendations with cited sources.

The first vertical — hydraulic filtration — is live at filteroracle.com. The platform is free for engineers. No account required to get a recommendation.

Vendor-neutral means vendor-neutral. Recommendations are based on operating conditions, engineering standards, and real-world performance data — not commercial relationships. This is what makes engineers trust it. It is also what makes the outcome data meaningful.

The exhaust

Every consultation generates data. Most of it flows back to the engineer — a recommendation, cited sources, a commissioning report. Some of it accumulates in the platform. We call this the exhaust: the structured byproduct of a useful service.

The exhaust has two layers.

Selection data

  • What engineers are selecting and why
  • Which operating conditions drive which product categories
  • Where engineers struggle — questions with no confident answer
  • Which specifications engineers cite most often
  • Which alternatives engineers consider and reject

Outcome data

  • Whether the recommended component was actually installed
  • Initial and ongoing performance readings (ΔP, cleanliness codes, etc.)
  • Whether the original problem was resolved
  • Total service life and failure mode, if applicable
  • "Would you select the same component again?" — answered by the engineer who lived with it

Outcome data is collected through the Component Performance Verification Protocol (CPVP) — a structured three-stage follow-up system that runs 24 hours, 4–6 weeks, and 6–12 months after installation. Each interaction delivers a professional commissioning report to the engineer. The outcome data is a byproduct of that service.

Why this data is different

Manufacturers have access to specification data — what their products are rated to do under controlled conditions. Distributors have access to sales data — what was ordered, when, and where. Neither has systematic access to what actually happened after installation.

What you have now

  • Performance in controlled test conditions
  • Sales volumes and distributor orders
  • Warranty claims (failures that were reported)
  • Customer satisfaction surveys (self-selected, infrequent)
  • Anecdotal field feedback through sales reps

What FluidOracle captures

  • Performance in real operating conditions, across real applications
  • Baseline and progressive performance readings over time
  • Failure modes in actual field conditions with operating context
  • Direct engineer judgment: would they specify it again?
  • Applications where your products are being selected and why
  • Applications where your products are being passed over and why

The most valuable data point in the system is an engineer who has run your product for nine months in a specific application answering a single question: "Knowing what you know now, would you select the same component for this application?" No specification, no test report, and no sales figure tells you what that answer tells you.

The compounding problem you should know about

Outcome data compounds. The platform that has been collecting structured field performance data for three years knows things that a platform that started collecting last month cannot know — regardless of how good the newer platform's AI is. There is no shortcut to having accumulated data for three years.

This matters for manufacturers because it determines which platform's recommendations engineers will trust over time. Recommendations grounded in real-world outcome data will eventually outperform recommendations grounded only in specifications — not because the AI is smarter, but because the data is better.

The manufacturers whose products are in FluidOracle's knowledge base, whose performance data is being captured, and who have a relationship with the platform will be positioned differently from those who don't — as that data accumulates.

What a manufacturer relationship looks like

FluidOracle does not sell featured placement in recommendations. Recommendations are determined by operating conditions and performance data, not commercial relationships. This is non-negotiable — it is the foundation of engineer trust, and engineer trust is what makes the outcome data meaningful.

What manufacturer relationships do provide:

  • Access to outcome data for your products — aggregated and anonymized, covering real-world field performance across applications
  • Knowledge base priority — your current catalog, application notes, and technical literature are kept current in the platform's retrieval system
  • Selection signal data — aggregated insight into when and why engineers are specifying your products, and when they're choosing alternatives
  • Early access to emerging verticals — as the platform expands to pumps, valves, seals, and other component categories
  • Performance gap identification — when real-world data diverges from specification data, you find out — before it becomes a warranty pattern

What we're looking for in manufacturer partners

We're building relationships with manufacturers who want to understand real-world field performance — not just sell. The right conversation starts with:

  • Current product catalog and application documentation
  • Willingness to receive outcome data, including unflattering results
  • Interest in understanding where real-world performance diverges from specification

We're not looking for endorsements, exclusivity, or co-marketing arrangements. We're looking for manufacturers who believe that better field data is good for the industry and good for their engineering credibility.
