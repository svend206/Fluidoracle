Abstract

Engineers who select industrial fluid-path components — filters, pumps, valves, seals — make decisions under uncertainty. Manufacturer specifications describe performance under controlled test conditions. Real installations are not controlled. Pressure spikes, fluid degradation, cold starts, contaminated new oil, and maintenance gaps create conditions that specifications cannot fully predict.

When components perform differently than expected — for better or worse — that information almost never returns to the people who recommended them, the engineers who will face the same decision next year, or the manufacturers who could address real-world performance gaps. The selection process is one-directional. Recommendations flow from specifications to engineers. Outcomes flow nowhere.

This paper introduces the Component Performance Verification Protocol (CPVP) — a structured framework for capturing post-installation outcome data through three follow-up interactions at defined intervals. CPVP is designed as an open standard: any platform, organization, or engineering team that makes component recommendations can implement it. The specification is published at fluidoracle.com/cpvp/v1/ under CC BY 4.0.

1. The problem

The industrial component selection process has a structural information gap. An engineer selects a hydraulic filter, a centrifugal pump, or a shaft seal based on available data: manufacturer catalogs, application guidelines, engineering standards, and the accumulated experience of colleagues. The selection is made, the component is installed, and the system runs.

Three months later, the filter element collapses during a cold start. Or the pump cavitates at a flow rate within its rated range. Or, more often, everything works exactly as expected — and the engineer has no mechanism to report that either.

Neither outcome propagates. The engineer who made the selection doesn't know what happened unless they personally follow up. The manufacturer whose product was specified doesn't know whether the installation was a success. The engineer at a different plant facing the same selection problem next year starts from the same place: specifications, guidelines, and whatever their colleagues happen to remember.

"A 55-gallon barrel of hydraulic oil contaminated with 500 milligrams of dust — the size of a single aspirin tablet — will not pass ISO cleanliness requirements for most hydraulic systems." — Schroeder Industries, Hydraulic Filtration Reference Guide

Engineers understand this. They learn, over years of experience, which specifications are conservative, which manufacturers' claims hold up in practice, and which applications require more margin than the data suggests. But that knowledge lives in people, not systems. It transfers imperfectly through mentorship and not at all across organizational boundaries.

The information exists — in the collective experience of the engineers who lived with the components. It is simply never collected.

2. The cost of silence

The consequences of this information gap are distributed and easy to miss in any single instance, but substantial in aggregate.

Recommendations without feedback loops

A recommendation that cannot be validated against outcomes cannot improve. Without outcome data, the same selection logic is applied indefinitely — including the same systematic errors. An AI platform trained on manufacturer specifications will reproduce manufacturer specification optimism unless grounded in real-world performance data.

Preventable repeat failures

Most component failures have identifiable root causes: under-specified filtration, insufficient collapse pressure margin, fluid incompatibility, cold-start conditions that exceed element ratings. When these failures are not captured in a structured way, the same failure recurs — sometimes at the same facility, sometimes across an industry.

Lost engineering knowledge

Senior engineers retire carrying decades of application-specific component experience. A maintenance engineer who has run the same hydraulic system for fifteen years knows exactly which filter elements hold up and which ones don't — in their specific contamination environment, with their specific fluid, under their specific operating cycle. None of this is in any specification. When that engineer leaves, the knowledge leaves with them.

Manufacturer accountability gaps

When performance claims cannot be validated against real-world outcomes, the market cannot efficiently distinguish manufacturers whose products perform as specified from those whose products do not. The result is pressure on price rather than performance — a well-documented dynamic in markets with high information asymmetry.

3. What CPVP is

The Component Performance Verification Protocol is a structured post-installation follow-up system. It defines three check-in interactions at defined intervals after a component is installed, each capturing progressively deeper performance data through conversation rather than forms.

CPVP is not a warranty program, a failure reporting system, or a customer satisfaction survey. It is an outcome tracking infrastructure — designed to capture what actually happened, grounded in the specific operating context of each installation, in a format that is useful to the engineer, not just the platform.

Two design decisions distinguish CPVP from conventional feedback systems:

Engineer-controlled timing. The commissioning clock starts when the engineer confirms installation — not when the recommendation was made. An engineer who reviews a recommendation but doesn't act for six weeks is not behind schedule. The protocol tracks the component lifecycle, not the consultation date.

Conversation, not forms. Each check-in is a conversation that picks up where the original consultation left off. The system knows what was recommended, why it was recommended, and what operating conditions were described. The engineer does not need to re-explain their system. The conversation can probe intelligently based on actual responses, deliver interpretation and value in real time, and capture information the engineer volunteers that no form would have thought to ask.

4. Design principles

  • Engineer-controlled timing The commissioning clock starts at installation confirmation, not at consultation. The protocol tracks component lifecycle, not calendar time from recommendation.
  • Conversation over forms Check-ins are conversations that adapt to what actually happened. A form with a wrong field fails silently. A conversation fails loudly and productively.
  • Value during check-in Each check-in delivers something useful to the engineer — interpretation of readings, trend projections, contextual advice. Data collection is a byproduct of a useful interaction, not the interaction itself.
  • Minimum quality bar A CPVP check-in must be better than a fresh conversation with no context. The grounding in the specific consultation record — operating conditions, component selected, reasoning — achieves this bar before asking a single question.
  • Partial data is valid Incomplete check-ins are captured and stored. An engineer who answers two questions and stops has contributed more than one who never responded. Partial data is better than no data.
  • Append-only records Check-in records are never overwritten. Corrections add dated notes to prior stage data rather than replacing it. The historical record is preserved.
  • Trust by design Every follow-up email must reference something only the platform would know. The engineer should recognize the follow-up immediately, before opening it.
  • PDF as primary deliverable Each check-in produces a professional commissioning report the engineer can file, share with plant management, or attach to a work order. Value delivery is not contingent on the engineer clicking through.

5. The protocol

Before the three main stages, a lightweight pre-commissioning contact is sent 48–72 hours after the initial consultation. Its purpose is not data collection — it is to maintain the thread and let the engineer self-select into the commissioning track. It acknowledges the consultation, references the specific recommendation, and asks simply whether the engineer moved forward. If the engineer confirms installation, the commissioning clock starts. If there is no response after a two-week follow-up, contact ceases. The protocol does not become noise.

Stage Timing Purpose Primary data
0 — Pre-commissioning 48–72 hours post-consultation Confirm installation intent; start commissioning clock Installation confirmation date
1 — Installation Record 24 hours post-installation Capture installation baseline while fresh Component installed, initial performance readings, old component condition
2 — Performance Verification 4–6 weeks post-installation Capture early performance with trend data Current readings vs. baseline, problem resolution status, anomalies
3 — Service Life Record 6–12 months post-installation Capture long-term reliability and the key judgment Total service life, failure mode, original problem resolution, would-select-again

For non-responding engineers at Stages 1, 2, and 3: one gentle follow-up after one week, then silence. Non-response is recorded as a data point — useful for understanding engagement rates and participation patterns across application types and industries.

The key question — Stage 3
"Knowing what you know now, would you select the same component for this application?"

This question, asked at Stage 3, is the single most valuable data point CPVP captures. It is a holistic judgment that incorporates everything the engineer experienced: installation difficulty, initial performance, reliability, cost against value, manufacturer support, and how well the component held up against conditions that may have differed from what was described in the original consultation. It cannot be inferred from specifications. It can only come from an engineer who lived with the component.

6. Reference scenario: hydraulic filtration

The following scenario illustrates a complete CPVP interaction for a hydraulic filtration application. The application is composite, drawn from real maintenance engineering contexts.

Application context Hydraulic filtration

System: Hydraulic power unit, automotive parts stamping press, 60-gallon reservoir

Problem: Three proportional valve failures in twelve months (~$4,000 per failure in parts and downtime). Colleague suggested the return-line filter may be inadequate.

Recommendation: Upgrade from spin-on 10-micron nominal element to a high-efficiency return-line filter assembly with a 3-micron absolute element (β₃(c) ≥ 1000). Target cleanliness: ISO 16/14/11 for proportional valve reliability. Reasoning: the 10-micron nominal element was likely passing particles in the 5–15 micron range — the range most damaging to proportional valve spools, which have critical clearances of 1–4 microns.

Stage 1 — Installation check-in (24 hours after installation)

Conversation excerpt — Stage 1
FluidOracle

Welcome back. You explored return-line filtration for your stamping press HPU and we discussed upgrading to a 3-micron absolute element. Did you end up going with that element, or something different?

Engineer

Went with it. Installed it yesterday during our Saturday shutdown.

FluidOracle

Good. A few things worth capturing while they're fresh — these become your baseline for tracking how the filter performs over the coming months. What's your initial differential pressure reading across the filter at operating temperature?

Engineer

About 12 psi at normal running temp.

FluidOracle

That's a reasonable starting point for that element at your flow rate with AW 46. When you opened the old housing, what did the old element look like? Any metal particles, sludge, or water?

Engineer

Lots of fine dark sludge. No obvious metal. Element was pretty much solid black.

FluidOracle

That's consistent with what you'd expect — the 10-micron nominal element was accumulating the particles it could capture while passing the fines straight through. Dark sludge suggests oxidized oil and fine wear debris built up over time. Did you pull a fluid sample before or after the swap?

Engineer

No, we don't really do fluid sampling. Should we?

FluidOracle

For a system with proportional valves, periodic sampling is one of the most cost-effective things you can do. A baseline sample in the next day or two and another at four to six weeks will show you exactly how much cleaner the system is getting. Most labs charge $25–40 per sample. Against $4,000 valve failures, it's cheap insurance.

Stage 1 produces the first section of the commissioning report: component confirmed, initial DP baseline of 12 psi recorded, old element condition documented, fluid sampling recommended. The PDF is generated at the close of the conversation and attached to the Stage 2 email.

Stage 2 — Performance verification (5 weeks later)

Conversation excerpt — Stage 2
FluidOracle

It's been about five weeks since you installed the new element on your stamping press HPU. How's it running?

Engineer

Pretty good. No valve issues since the swap.

FluidOracle

That's an encouraging early sign. What's your current differential pressure reading?

Engineer

Showing about 18 psi.

FluidOracle

You've gone from 12 to 18 psi in five weeks — a moderate loading rate. The element is doing its job. Bypass on that housing typically opens around 30–35 psi, so at your current rate you're looking at an element change around weeks 12–15. Loading rate usually slows as the system gets cleaner, so that projection may be conservative. Did you end up pulling a fluid sample?

Engineer

Yeah, we pulled one last week. Lab said ISO 17/15/12.

FluidOracle

That's a significant improvement for a system that was failing proportional valves. You were likely running ISO 20/18/15 or worse before the change — each ISO code step is a doubling of particle count. 17/15/12 is at the threshold for proportional valve reliability. As the system continues to clean up, 16/14/11 is achievable and would give you real margin. No unusual noise, temperature changes, or anomalies?

Engineer

Nope, running normal.

Stage 3 — Service life record (11 months later)

Conversation excerpt — Stage 3
FluidOracle

It's been about eleven months since you installed the new element on your stamping press HPU. Is it still in service?

Engineer

Replaced it about two months ago. Got to about 28 psi and decided to swap it on a scheduled shutdown.

FluidOracle

So roughly nine months of service life, changed proactively before bypass — good practice. What did the element look like when you pulled it?

Engineer

Dark but even loading, no collapse, no bypass marks.

FluidOracle

Even loading with no structural damage means the element was correctly sized for your flow and contamination level. How have the proportional valves been over the full period?

Engineer

Zero failures since the filter swap. That's the longest we've gone in three years.

FluidOracle

Eleven months, zero valve failures, versus three in the previous twelve months. That's approximately $12,000 in avoided failures. Knowing what you know now, would you select the same filter for this application?

Engineer

Absolutely. Already ordered replacement elements.

The final commissioning report — four sections, covering fourteen months from initial consultation to service life record — is generated and delivered to the engineer. It documents baseline DP, loading trajectory, fluid cleanliness improvement, nine months of service life, zero valve failures, and an explicit confirmation that the engineer would select the same component again. It is a document the engineer can file in their maintenance system, show to plant management, or reference the next time the same question arises.

It also answers, definitively, whether the recommendation was correct.

7. Email and trust

Engineers at industrial facilities often have aggressive email filtering and a well-calibrated skepticism toward unsolicited messages. CPVP makes specific requirements for follow-up email design, because a technically sound protocol that engineers don't trust or don't open is not a protocol at all.

Subject lines must be specific. Not "How's your filter working?" but "Your 3-micron return-line element in stamping press HPU — installation check-in." The engineer should recognize the follow-up before opening it — ideally before deciding whether to open it.

The email body summarizes the original consultation. Application context, component recommended or installed, date of consultation, key operating parameters. Sufficient information to confirm legitimacy without requiring a click. The engineer who has no time to click through today should still receive value from the email itself.

The PDF commissioning report is attached to every Stage 1, 2, and 3 email alongside the check-in link. Engineers can file the PDF, forward it to a colleague, or attach it to a work order regardless of whether they engage with the conversation. Maximum value delivery with minimum friction.

Tokenized links are time-limited, not single-use. A seven-day expiry with a re-authentication fallback accommodates the reality that engineers get interrupted. Single-use tokens that expire on first click are a form design optimized for the platform, not the engineer.

DKIM, SPF, and DMARC authentication are required. Non-negotiable for deliverability into industrial facility mail environments. The sending domain, link domain, and platform brand must be consistent. Domain changes reset trust with both mail providers and individual engineers.

8. The data model

CPVP defines a minimum data model that implementing platforms must capture. Vertical-specific extensions are expected and encouraged — the base model provides the shared structure that makes cross-vertical and cross-platform aggregation possible.

Two architectural principles govern the data model:

Operating context is system-level, not vertical-level. The hydraulic fluid, system pressure, flow rate, temperature range, and application description captured during a filtration consultation describe the hydraulic system — not just the filter. When the same engineer later asks about a pump on the same power unit, that context already exists. The platform is not starting from scratch. This is the architectural foundation that makes CPVP useful across verticals.

Records are append-only. When an engineer reports at Stage 2 that what was actually installed differed from Stage 1, the record does not update — it appends a dated correction. The historical record is preserved. This is not a limitation; it is an intentional design decision that makes the data trustworthy over time.

9. The compounding value of outcome data

A single CPVP record — one engineer, one component, one installation — is moderately useful. It validates or refutes one recommendation. It documents one failure mode or one success.

The value of CPVP is not in individual records. It is in what the records reveal in aggregate, over time.

100
outcomes → identify which components perform in high-temperature applications versus what their spec sheets predict
1K
outcomes → identify which manufacturers' performance claims consistently match real-world results across applications
10K
outcomes → a performance database that no manufacturer, distributor, or standards body possesses

Each outcome data point makes future recommendations better. This compounds: recommendations improve, which increases engineer trust, which increases engagement and outcome reporting, which further improves recommendations. The asset grows with use. This dynamic is not achievable with manufacturer specifications alone, because specifications describe controlled conditions. It is only achievable with real-world outcome data collected systematically from real installations.

The component selection platform that accumulates outcome data will, over time, know things about real-world component performance that no manufacturer, distributor, or standards body knows. That knowledge is not a feature. It is a competitive position that compounds and cannot be replicated by starting later.

This is why CPVP is built into the platform from day one, not added as a later feature. The value of outcome data is proportional to its age and volume. There is no shortcut to having collected data for three years. The accumulation begins only when the infrastructure begins.

10. Implementing CPVP

CPVP is designed to work across any component category. Implementing platforms register vertical-specific configuration — check-in parameters, performance metrics, failure mode taxonomies — and the shared infrastructure handles scheduling, email delivery, conversation flow, and PDF generation.

For the hydraulic filtration vertical, the key performance metrics registered are: differential pressure (baseline, current, loading rate, projected life); fluid cleanliness code (ISO 4406); problem resolution status (valve failures, contamination events, or whatever the engineer reported as their original problem); and component condition at replacement. The failure mode taxonomy includes: media fouling, housing corrosion, differential pressure exceedance, bypass, media migration, mechanical damage, and changed system requirements.

The minimum requirements for a compliant CPVP implementation:

  • Three check-in stages at the specified intervals, triggered by installation confirmation
  • Append-only records with dated corrections
  • Email trust requirements met: subject line specificity, consultation summary in body, DKIM/SPF/DMARC authentication, unsubscribe in every message
  • The Stage 3 key question asked and recorded
  • A PDF commissioning report generated and delivered to the engineer at each stage

The full specification, including data model definitions and vertical configuration guidance, is available at fluidoracle.com/cpvp/v1/ under CC BY 4.0. Organizations implementing CPVP are encouraged to contact checkins@fluidoracle.com to be listed as implementing platforms and to contribute to future versions of the standard.

11. Conclusion

The industrial component selection process has a structural gap that has persisted because it is difficult to close without purpose-built infrastructure. Engineers make decisions, components are installed, systems run — and the outcomes flow nowhere. The knowledge accumulates in people and dissipates when those people leave.

CPVP does not solve this with a survey or a feedback form. It closes the loop through structured follow-up conversations grounded in the specific context of each installation, at intervals calibrated to the component lifecycle, delivering value to the engineer at every interaction. The outcome data is a byproduct of a useful service, not an extraction in exchange for nothing.

The protocol is simple. The infrastructure to run it is not trivial. But the compounding value of the data it produces — validated over time, across applications, across component categories — represents something the industrial engineering community does not currently have: a systematic, structured record of how components actually perform in the field.

We are publishing CPVP as an open standard because the problem it addresses is an industry problem, not a platform problem. The more organizations that implement it, the more valuable the aggregate dataset becomes. We invite engineers, manufacturers, distributors, and platform builders to implement CPVP, to use it, and to contribute to its evolution.
